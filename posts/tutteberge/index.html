Tutte-Berge Theorem

Given a graph $ G = (V,E) $, for vertex-matching $M \subseteq E$, the following equality holds \[ \max_{ \textrm{matching } M} |M| = \min_{U \subseteq V}\frac{|U| + |V| - odd\left( G[V \setminus U] \right)}{2} \] where $odd(G)$ is the number of odd-vertexed connected components of the graph $G$. The non-unique $U$ satisfying the equality is called the Tutte witness.

This formula gives us an exact size of the max matching in a general graph $G$. In particular, this is a general analogue of KÃ¶nig’s Theorem for Bipartite Graphs, which gives us an equivalence between the size of the max matching and the min vertex cover in a bipartite graph.

There are poly-time algorithms to find a max matching in a bipartite graph $\implies$ finding the size of a minimum vertex cover is a P problem in bipartite graphs! - this is easy to see but I find it cool to have the same result from different directions.

Proof sketch:

  1. The $\le$ side of the equality follows from the inequality for every matching and every $U \subseteq V$

  2. Now to show tightness, consider inducing over $|V| = n$. It is trivial that for $n = 0$, the max matching and the RHS are both nil.

    Assume the equality holds for $n-1$. Assume $G$ is connected else induce also over the number of connected components of $G$.

    a.
    Suppose there exists a vertex $v$ matched in every max matching. Then, \[ \max_{\textrm{matching } M’ \textrm{ in } G\setminus \{v\} } |M’| = \max_{\textrm{matching } M \textrm{ in } G} |M| - 1 \]

    By the inductive hypothesis, the LHS here is equal to

    \[ \frac{|U’| + |V| - odd(G[V \setminus U’])}{2} \textrm{ for some } U’ \subseteq V \setminus \{v\} \textrm{ by minimality } \]

    Let $U = U’ \cup \{ v\} \subseteq V$, then \[ \begin{align*} \max_{M} |M| &= \max_{M’} |M’| + 1 \\ &= \frac{|U’| + |V \setminus \{v\}| - odd(G[V \setminus U])}{2} + 1 \\ &= \frac{(|U’| + 1) + (|V \setminus \{v\}| + 1) - odd(G[V \setminus U])}{2} \\ &= \frac{|U| + |V| - odd\left( G[V \setminus U] \right)}{2} \blacksquare \end{align*} \]

    b.

    Suppose there isn’t a vertex $v$ matched in every matching. In this case, there isn’t a perfect matching in $G$. \[ |M| < \frac12 |V| \] We can show in this case that there exists a matching of size $ \frac12 (|V| - 1) $ and so the theorem only holds when $U = \emptyset$.

    Suppose not: every max matching $M$ misses $\ge 2$ vertices $s_M, t_M$. Among all such $(M, s_M, t_M)$, choose one that minimizes $\textrm{dist}(s_M, t_M)$, let these be known as $M, s, t$.

    Case 1: $\textrm{dist}(s,t) = 1$, then these are adjacent vertices which are both not paired, and thus the edge $\{s,t\}$ can be added to $M$, which is a contradiction to the maximality of $M$.

    Case 2: $\textrm{dist}(s,t) \ge 2$, choose $u$ on $s \to t$. Note that $u$ is matched else the distance is not the shortest. There must exist a max matching $N$ where $u$ is left exposed. Among all such $N$, choose one to maximize $| M \cap N| $.

    Note by the triangle inequality that $\textrm{dist}(s,u) < \textrm{dist}(s, t)$ and $\textrm{dist}(u,t) < \textrm{dist}(s, t)$. Thus, $s,t$ are covered by $N$ else we have a contradiction to our choices for $M, s, t$.

    Since $M,N$ are both max matchings, we have that they cover the same number of vertices. So, there exists a $x \neq u$ covered by $M$ and exposed by $N$. Thus, there must exist a $y$ such that $\{x,y\} \in M$ - can this $y$ be exposed in $N$? No, otherwise we could include $\{x,y\}$ in $N$, a contradiction to maximality of $N$. Thus, there exists a $z$ such that $\{y,z\} \in N$.

    Now note that replacing $N$ with $N’ = N \cup \{x,y\} \setminus \{y,z\}$ yields a max matching which misses $u$ and such that \[ |M \cap N’| > |M \cap N|\]

    which is a contradiction to the choice of $N$. $\blacksquare$
